Residents of Jinack Island Call for Alternatives as Cannabis Farms Destroyed
General News
By Assan Bah
For many residents of Jinack Island, encompassing the villages of Kajatta and Niggi, survival has become increasingly challenging amid decades of economic neglect, climate change, and limited development opportunities. These conditions, locals say, have forced several families into illegal cannabis cultivation, an activity that historically was never part of the island’s culture.
The situation drew national attention after law enforcement agencies destroyed at least sixty cannabis farms on the island in late February and early March 2026. While authorities cited compliance with the Drug Control Act, 2003, residents insist that the root causes of cultivation, which are economic hardship and limited alternatives, must be addressed.
Speaking from Jinack Kajatta, the village Alkalo, Mr. Mamadou Manneh, said, “We have three pressing issues to negotiate with government, but we want to be very careful. We don’t want to be disappointed. Our fear is that the government may turn against Jinack and block us into poverty.”
He called for the government to reconsider plans to designate Jinack’s entire land as a park and wildlife area, arguing that, “It’s not a good decision and has no reward to the government. We want to use our land resources to cultivate and gain back our glory.”
Highlighting the critical water shortage, Manneh stressed, “However, we cannot do agricultural activities with the current water condition of Jinack. So it means we need fresh water. Can government pilot Jinack for their desalination programs? We have seen Senegal negotiating with Saudi Arabia, and we heard that even the Gambian government has expressed interest in desalination.” He emphasized that freshwater access is crucial for food security, especially for seasonal crops.
Manneh also appealed for protective measures against saltwater intrusion. “Can they bring us temporal pipe water from the mainland that allows people to farm and sustain their families? Without fresh water, land cultivation is zero per cent guaranteed,” he said. He drew parallels to coastal protection projects in West Coast and Banjul, insisting, “By doing that, we can have rain seasonal farming on our rice fields. This has to be protected and prevented from salt intrusion so we can use our swamp sites like CRR, Niumi, Aljamdu, and others.”
Asked about official dialogue with authorities, he noted, “Even though there has not been any engagement yet, we heard an announcement by the Minister of Information that government plans exist for us. But they just started destroying the cannabis fields last weekend. We are making this interview to bring our natural history to the people of Gambia, to show why Jinack is vulnerable, and what we expect from the state. We are waiting for engagement.”
In Jinack Niggi, Alkalo Bubacarr Bojang echoed the challenges. “We only started cannabis cultivation as a means of livelihood after failing to grow other crops. Security operatives recently came here and destroyed everything without any explanation. They never held any discussion with us, and we heard no information from them.”
He expressed frustration with living conditions, suggesting that some residents are considering moving to Senegal. “For now, we cannot ask anything from the government. We are patiently waiting for alternatives because they say we must stop cannabis cultivation by destroying our farms,” he said.
Women on the island also proposed alternative livelihoods. One resident, speaking anonymously, recommended poultry or livestock farming for women, noting, “We depended on cashew, cannabis, and sometimes the river, which was closed by the Department of Wildlife.” She added that efforts to cultivate cassava are often hindered by cattle interference, urging authorities to provide fishing boats and fish processing sites.
Authorities have defended the crackdown. On February 28 and 1 March 2026, a joint operation by the Drug Law Enforcement Agency of The Gambia (DLEAG) and the Police Intervention Unit targeted the villages of Jinack Kajatta and Niggi, destroying at least 60 cannabis farms. DLEAG stated, “During the operation, sixty cannabis farms were destroyed on the island, representing a major disruption of this illicit trade. Cannabis cultivation is a flagrant violation of Section 36 of the Drug Control Act, 2003, which explicitly prohibits the cultivation of plants from which prohibited drugs can be obtained.”
The agency added that, “Guided by the Ministry of Interior, DLEAG will implement its mandate to control illicit drug trafficking and abuse through effective strategies, addressing drug-related crimes, and enhancing public safety and security.”
Residents, however, warn that enforcement without development support will only worsen economic hardship. As Manneh concluded, “We are not asking for special favors. We want the government to help us use our land resources wisely and provide basic infrastructure like water and protective measures so we can survive without breaking the law.”

Victims’ Centre Hails ‘Jungler’ Michael Sang Correa’s Sentencing as ‘Victory’ for Survivors
Human Rights
The Gambia Center for Victims of Human Rights Violations (Victims’ Center) has hailed the sentencing of ‘Jungler’ Michael Sang Correa as a significant victory for survivors and families affected by atrocities committed during the Jammeh regime.
“The sentencing of Jungler Michael Sang Correa is a landmark moment in the global fight against impunity,” said the Victims’ Center in a statement. “The long arm of justice is reaching all those who perpetrated human rights violations in the Gambia.”
Correa is the second member of the Junglers to be convicted for crimes against humanity. His sentencing follows the life imprisonment of Bai Lowe in Germany and a 20-year sentence for Ousman Sonko in Switzerland. These convictions bring hope to victims and increase pressure on the Gambian government to pursue justice for those affected by Jammeh-era abuses.
The pursuit of accountability for Jammeh’s crimes continues internationally. The Gambian Truth, Reconciliation and Reparations Commission (TRRC) has called for the prosecution of Jammeh and 69 of his accomplices — many of whom have been arrested in Germany, Switzerland, and the United States. Jammeh himself remains in Equatorial Guinea.
The Victims’ Center expressed its gratitude to the U.S. prosecutors, the Criminal Court of Colorado, and the international community for their commitment to justice. “We will continue to fight and advocate for the rights of Gambians and Non-Gambians to ensure peace and justice,” the Center affirmed, pledging to uphold the spirit of the “NEVER AGAIN” campaign against future abuses.

About Us
Foroyaa was established in1987 and soon became known as the paper
that speaks the truth and promotes civic awareness. It is respected by all
sectors of society. Foroyaa's policy is to avoid speculation and get to the
truth through investigation before publication – publication of the truth in
good faith and in the public interest.
Foroyaa aims to play its constitutional role (section 207) of holding the
government accountable to the people to minimize impunity and abuse of
office that are often detrimental to the welfare of the people. It is at the
forefront in covering arrests, detention without trial, attacks on the media,
unconstitutional behaviour, misappropriation of public funds, misconduct
and all other forms of maladministration.
Foroyaa extensively focuses on governance issues.
The paper is read by people of all walks of life who are seeking information
or knowledge. Even those who oppose the opinions expressed
acknowledge that Foroyaa publishes facts and reports fairly.
Foroyaa is legally registered as a newspaper and incorporated as a
company. It has an Editorial Board of six members that is responsible for
policy matters. Its offices are located in the Kanifing Muncipality, where
nearly all the newspapers and most radio stations are based, is run by the
managing editor who is the secretary to the Board.
Its staff number 32 (including 7 media practitioners). It also engages the
services of 12 freelance reporters and columnists and 7 others as
newspaper distributors. Foroyaa relies on proceeds from sales and
advertisements to run the newspaper.
Foroyaa has been operating a website since 2009 and the website has
been reconstructed and now provides for an online radio and many other
features or applications.
In addition to the newspaper, Foroyaa monthly magazine started
publication in February 2018.
Foroyaa has also applied for a licence to operate an FM radio station which
will streamline its broadcast.

61 Years After Independence, Jinack Residents Still Buy Drinking Water From Senegal
Latest News
Burning Issues
By Assan Bah
Residents and local leaders on Jinack Island in the Lower Niumi District of the North Bank Region have told
Foroyaa
that one of the island’s two settlements still depends on purchased water from neighbouring Senegal for drinking, more than six decades after The Gambia gained independence.
Community members say Jinack Niggi, one of the two settlements on the island, continues to rely on buying clean drinking water across the border because local wells are either salty or inadequate. The other settlement on the island is Kajatta.
The two communities are said to have historical links with neighbouring Senegal, with Jinack Niggi tracing its origins to Jinack Jattaco and Kajatta to Jinack Barra.
Residents say the island, surrounded by the Atlantic Ocean and intersected by more than 50 river streams, was once considered rich in natural resources before climate change effects began to affect livelihoods in the 1970s. Around the same time, the government designated the island as a park and wildlife area — a decision some islanders argue discouraged investment and development.
Bubacarr Bojang, the seventh alkalo of Jinack Niggi, said successive governments since independence have made little meaningful investment in the community.
“We buy the water that we drink from neighboring communities in Senegal,” he said.
“If I give you the water we drink you can’t take it. If you look at the well you say when you die when drink that water. In the Gambia, which village buy their drinking water from Senegal? It’s only in Jinack,” he added, stating that although authorities are aware of the situation, nothing has been done to address it.
Despite what he described as decades of neglect, Bojang said politicians continue to visit the island during election campaigns.
“They come with nice promises but nothing,” he said. “When it’s time for paying tax we pay but we don’t see any development here.”
He further described the lack of basic infrastructure in the community, including roads, electricity and potable water.
According to him, even buying basic food supplies such as rice involves costly transport by boat to other areas.
“Purchasing a bag of rice requires costly transport by boat to other areas, while smaller local purchases remain expensive, particularly for those with limited economic means. If you are not economically strong, you buy per cup from the local shops which is also very expensive,” he said.
The absence of electricity also affects everyday life.
“To send your child to the shop at night you give him or her a torch light otherwise they cannot go out,” he added.
Mamadou Manneh, alkalo of Jinack Kajatta, said saltwater intrusion has worsened the island’s water crisis.
“Our water wells doesn’t contain water more than 60 litres to 100 litres,” he said, noting that the sandy terrain makes digging for water difficult and often forces residents to abandon wells after repeated attempts.
“People desperately search for water,” he lamented.
Manneh said the only direct government investment in water infrastructure on the island dates back to 1987, when a well was constructed with funding from the government.
“A local well of less than five metre depth built in 1987 on the FRG fund and all the FRG wells have got hand pumps except the well of Jinack which is the only water resource of Jinack provided by the state. This is the only well in Kajatta without hand pump. Niggi doesn’t even get it,” he said.
He also noted that most development projects in the community have been undertaken by residents themselves.
“Our school was established by the community, with all structures constructed through their efforts. In 2009, a sponsor helped build a modern clinic and a top-tier school structure,” he said, adding that the government later built a five-bedroom teachers’ staff quarters.
Manneh also recalled that during the administration of former President Sir Dawda Jawara, residents were asked to clear land in preparation for a road and bridge project intended to connect the island more easily with Barra.
“Sir Dawda asked for people to clear the road, asked his area council in Kerewan to prepare for a feeder road to Jinack and then bridge at Barra,” he said.
“This is real and the two communities even did the clearing. The area council came and put up a wooden bridge at Barra, which is supposed to be concrete.”
However, he said the project stalled after a policy decision was later taken to designate Jinack as a state reserve area.
“Before the court’s decision, it was revealed that Jinack would become a park, possibly leading those responsible for building the bridge and road to believe their work was unnecessary. It is very sad. Now we are paying the most expensive transport because of that,” he said.
Residents say the absence of a proper road means that although Jinack lies only about 14 kilometres from Barra, transportation costs remain among the highest in the country. Local riders reportedly charge around D250, with reduced fares sometimes offered only to residents.
Manneh said a philanthropist once attempted to build a feeder road to the island, but the Department of Parks and Wildlife publicly opposed the project.
The difficult terrain and lack of transport infrastructure also affect access to healthcare.
“In this community, many people are born while en-route to the hospital,” Manneh said, explaining that pregnant women sometimes walk up to 30 kilometres to reach the nearest health facility.
Communication services are also limited due to the absence of reliable mobile network infrastructure.
“Currently, residents struggle to connect with new phones. What the VDC are telling me is that they are always saying the number, the population in the islands. It’s not up to their target to put a tower for us,” he said.
Manneh urged the government to consider making Jinack a pilot development project or to extend a water pipeline from the mainland.
“The water supply here, in fact, you are seeing the pipe water that we are drinking, is also a private donor, funded from our mainland, with no expatriate, work applied on it,” he said, noting that the system often becomes contaminated with saltwater and residents must repair frequent pipe bursts themselves.
“Our situation is very sad,” he stressed.
Residents say electricity generation on the island has largely been handled by the community itself.
Manneh said the community has spent seven years working toward electrification and is now expecting a renewable energy project supported by the government and the United Nations Development Programme (UNDP).
According to him, the project to electrify both settlements is expected to begin in March.
Lamin I. Jatta, a youth from Jinack Niggi, said access to the island itself is challenging due to limited routes.
“The beach side is inaccessible during high tides, and the land route requires over two hours of travel through bush paths. Additionally, vehicles operate only once daily, departing in the morning and returning between 5 to 8 PM,” he said.
“These vehicles sometimes have mechanical problems in the bush only for us to take a fishing boats to rescue them because the whole route is sandy,” he added.
Jatta also described the difficulty residents face in accessing mobile network coverage.
“To get mobile network, you leave all this area and go down the river because there no tower or antenna here. Everything here is hard,” he said.
A woman who spoke on condition of anonymity said the community’s isolation places an additional burden on women.
“A ‘Jakarta’ to Barra some charge D150 and costs us nothing less than D300 a day. Women are really suffering here. There is no market, garage, or lumo we only have boutiques which is expensive,” she said.
“To get clean drinking water, we go all the way to Jinack Barra to buy because our water salty,” she added.

Former Gam-Petroleum General Manager Denies Financial Dealings With Gach Global in Multi-Million...
Latest News
Legal Affairs
By Mariama Marong
The former General Manager of Gam‑Petroleum, Saikou Drammeh, on Wednesday denied having any monetary dealings with Gach Global Trading Company while testifying in an ongoing court case involving business disputes linked to petroleum transactions.
Drammeh made the statement during cross-examination before a court in The Gambia on March 11, where he was questioned by defence lawyer K. Drammeh, representing the first respondent, Kaddijatou Kebbeh.
In the matter before the court, Kebbeh is listed as the first respondent while the second respondent is represented by lawyer B. Conteh. The plaintiff, businessman Abubacar Jawara, is represented by counsel I. Drammeh.
During his testimony, Drammeh told the court that during his time at Gam-Petroleum, operational and structural reports were routinely produced, although he noted that there was no clear structural framework in place as far back as 2008.
The witness explained that he joined Gam-Petroleum as an operations officer and later rose through the ranks within the company. According to him, he became acting general manager in 2016 before being confirmed as the substantive general manager in 2017, a position he held until 2021.
He told the court that in that capacity he was responsible for overseeing operations and reporting directly to the managing director while working alongside a senior operations manager.
Drammeh also confirmed that he knew the plaintiff, Abubacar Jawara, describing him as a businessman with whom he interacted in the course of his duties as general manager of Gam-Petroleum.
When asked by counsel K. Jallow whether he had any dealings with Gach Global Trading Company, Drammeh acknowledged that the company had approached Gam-Petroleum seeking a shortage facility arrangement that would allow petroleum products to be stored alongside supplies belonging to the National Water and Electricity Company.
However, he insisted that no payments were made under the arrangement after the agreement was reached.
“After the agreement was made, there was no payment on the contract,” Drammeh told the court, maintaining that Gam-Petroleum did not sell products under that shortage facility arrangement.
He further emphasized that Gam-Petroleum does not release payments solely on the basis of contracts and maintained that Gach Global Trading Company had not made any payment to the company under the arrangement.
Drammeh also informed the court that the Ghana National Petroleum Corporation was among the shareholders of Gam-Petroleum during the period he worked there.
When questioned about his relationship with Kaddijatou Kebbeh, the witness acknowledged that he knew her and identified her as an agent associated with Gach Global Trading Company.
He further confirmed that he later married Kebbeh, but said that at the time of their marriage she was not working with Gach Global.
Drammeh told the court that disputes involving Kebbeh and Gach Global had at one point been reported to the police.
He also confirmed that he had previously been arrested by state authorities in relation to diesel petroleum products, adding that during his detention he remained in contact with Kebbeh.
The witness also rejected suggestions that he had financial dealings with Gach Global through an associate identified as Abdoulie Sen.
During the proceedings, counsel K. Jallow requested permission from the court to play a video recording that allegedly contained the voice of Abdoulie Sen and showed footage of large sums of money in different currencies.
When asked about the video, Drammeh said he had seen the footage online and claimed the money shown in the recording was related to petroleum business activities.
Pressed further on when the video was recorded, the witness said it had been filmed at his private office after he had already left his position at Gam-Petroleum.
“The money was for petroleum business,” he told the court.
Under further questioning, Drammeh confirmed that his employment with Gam-Petroleum eventually ended after he refused to attend disciplinary hearings convened by the company.
“I was fired from Gam-Petroleum because I refused to attend disciplinary hearings at their office,” he said.
The case was adjourned and is scheduled to resume on April 30, 2026 from 10 a.m. to 12 noon, with further proceedings also set for May 6, 2026 from 12 noon to 2 p.m..

NAWEC Uncovers Over 25 Illegal Electrical Connections at Former President’s Garden in...
Latest News
Burning Issues
By Assan Bah
The National Water and Electricity Company (NAWEC) has uncovered more than two dozen alleged illegal electricity connections at the garden of former President Yahya A.J.J. Jammeh in Batokunku, a coastal village in the West Coast Region. The revelation, described by officials as part of a widespread and long-running problem, has triggered internal investigations and stern warnings to both the public and NAWEC’s own staff.
Addressing journalists on Tuesday, senior executives of the utility company described the discovery as part of a broader and alarming pattern of unauthorized connections that have, for years, undermined NAWEC’s operations nationwide.
“If NAWEC fails, we all fail,” said Managing Director Gallow Saidy during a press briefing at the site. “Some of these activities are not unique to this area. It’s across the country, and it needs to stop.”
Mr. Saidy, visibly frustrated by the scale of the issue, issued a blunt warning: individuals found culpable—whether members of the public or NAWEC staff—will face the full force of the law. According to him, illegal connections not only deprive the utility of revenue but also threaten the stability and safety of the national power grid.
The managing director emphasized that NAWEC has clear policies governing access to its services, and that anyone seeking electricity or water connections must go through official channels.
“If it is a NAWEC staff, we’ll deal with you—even take you to court. If it is from the public, we will equally do the same,” Saidy stated. “We will remove those meters, and we will not reconnect you, because what you have done is wrong.”
Mr. Saidy also attributed frequent low voltage and outages in some areas to such illegal connections, citing a system where one person connects illegally and others tap from that source, compounding technical losses.
“People may say, ‘Oh this is within NAWEC staff.’ Fine, but we are going to go after them,” he added. “It’s not business as usual.”
He accused certain individuals of using religious or moral pretense to shield dishonest behavior. “We go to the mosque and say we are praying, and then go and do things like this,” he said. “We will not leave any stone unturned.”
NAWEC officials said they are reviewing whether fines and surcharges are sufficient penalties. According to Mr. Saidy, the company may consider longer disconnections as a deterrent. “Maybe we don’t give you electricity for another six months,” he suggested. “We are going to do this across the country.”
He revealed that the company is also investing in investigative resources, including the use of external parties to trace illegal connections and identify those responsible.
“If we catch somebody here, we will internally get rid of them and then take them to the police,” Mr. Saidy declared. “NAWEC is not going to withdraw any case going forward.”
Babucarr Awe, NAWEC’s Senior Transmission and Distribution Manager, warned that the illegal practices are not only financially damaging but pose serious safety risks.
“These connections cause a series of instabilities, resulting in outages and low voltage,” Mr. Awe said. “Some of these setups are not standard and can lead to electrocutions, which are fatal.”
According to Mr. Awe, unauthorized installations can also cause surges and infrastructure failures. “Electricity is very dangerous,” he emphasized. “Some of these connections are done in ways that endanger both the user and people nearby.”
NAWEC’s Senior Customer Service Manager, Hassan Bah, explained that part of the challenge in tracing illegal meters lies in how they were originally registered. Many meters now operating in Batokunku, he said, were purchased under different community names—such as Tanje, Gunjur, Bakoteh, and even Serekunda—making them difficult to trace through standard records.
“They were moved from the place they were bought for,” Mr. Bah said. “This is the reason why we could not easily trace these meters.”
Momodou Dampha, the company’s Senior HR and Administration Manager, acknowledged the possibility of internal collusion, suggesting some employees might be complicit in the illegal activities.
“As a company with a moral responsibility, we are aware of the management component,” he said. “We will ensure that any employee involved in this illegal activity faces the full force of the law and of internal regulations.”
Tijan Williams, Group Director of Internal Audit, said investigators have so far discovered over 25 illegal meters at the Batokunku garden, with more than half already removed.
“It’s more than 25 that we have found here. We listed them,” he said. “We’ve removed more than half.”
He added that NAWEC has collected testimonies from individuals, including farmers who admitted to paying as much as D21,500 to acquire meters under questionable circumstances.
“So whom did they pay to?” Mr. Williams asked. “He will name the staff or name an agent, and then we book down all those things.”
All information gathered is being compiled into a final report for senior management and the NAWEC board to guide further disciplinary or legal action.
Mr. Saidy ended the press briefing with an appeal to the public: “If you see these things happening in your area, report them. We are not perfect. We need your help.”
He reminded customers that NAWEC’s mission is to provide safe, reliable, and equitable access to energy—but that goal cannot be achieved if the system is manipulated.
“Everybody is entitled to electricity, but not through the back way,” he said. “There will be no massage, no maslaha. We’ll deal with it.”

Scorpions’ boss pleased with win over Burundi
Latest News
Sports
Scorpions' boss Johnathan McKinstry. © GFF Media
By Momodou Jarju
The head coach of Scorpions Johnathan McKinstry told GFF Media that he was pleased with the 2-0 victory they secured against Burundi in the eighth round of the FIFA World Cup qualifiers. He also shared his elation with the Gambian community in Kenya who turned out in their numbers to support the players.
Commenting on the game, Johnny said they have huge confidence and faith in the squad which was why they rotated the starting line-up. McKinstry made four changes to the team that started in Friday’s 3-1 win over Kenya.
“[It’s] a different type of game, kicking off at 10 o’clock at night, floodlights going out for 30 minutes, it’s currently half past midnight and the game’s just finished. So this was a really crazy sort of situation to find ourselves in, but the guys have worked extremely hard, 2-0 win, a clean sheet, we denied Burundi, they had one or two chances, but a lot, we could have maybe scored another couple, so guys worked extremely hard.”
“This is the first time in the history of Gambian football that the Scorpions win back-to-back World Cup qualifiers. I’m not sure of the African qualifiers, but definitely the World Cup, this is the first time. Yeah, we were aware coming into this, look, they’ve had some back-to-back wins before, but they’ve been in different windows, so maybe they won the second game in a March window and won the first game in a June window, but we became aware earlier in the week that they’ve never won back-to-back qualifiers in the same window, so to have a perfect window of six points, it’s never happened in the last 60, 70 years of Gambian football, so we told that to the players.
“We consistently asked them to raise the standard, and yes, it puts them under pressure, because maybe you’d say, oh, don’t put them under pressure, they’re young guys, but I think these guys respond to pressure, and look, it’s not a qualification, it’s not playing at a World Cup, but I think we have to take every step forward and celebrate it, and we’ve got to strive for better every day,” he said while adding this result probably move The Gambia five, six, or seven places in the World Rankings.

China Calls for Ceasefire in Middle East as Beijing Prepares to Host...
General News
By Yankuba Jallow
China’s Foreign Minister, Wang Yi, on Saturday used a high-profile press conference to call for an immediate ceasefire in the escalating Middle East conflict while outlining Beijing’s ambitions to reshape regional economic cooperation at the upcoming Asia-Pacific Economic Cooperation Economic Leaders’ Meeting scheduled for November in Shenzhen.
Speaking on the sidelines of the Fourth Session of the 14th National People’s Congress in Beijing, Mr Wang addressed Chinese and international journalists on a range of foreign policy issues from the intensifying confrontation involving Iran, Israel and the United States to China’s plans for deepening economic integration across the Asia-Pacific.
In remarks that reflected Beijing’s growing diplomatic assertiveness, the veteran diplomat warned that the expanding hostilities in the Middle East risk plunging the region into deeper turmoil unless military operations cease immediately.
“This is a war that should not have happened,” Mr Wang said, describing the conflict as one that “does no one any good.”
He cautioned that the use of force would only worsen the situation, invoking an ancient Chinese maxim: “Weapons are ominous tools and should not be used without discretion.”
“As the Middle East is engulfed in flames,” he added, “China calls for an immediate stop to military operations to avoid spiralling escalation and prevent the conflict from spreading further.”
Mr Wang outlined what he described as five guiding principles that should underpin any solution to the tensions surrounding Iran and the wider region.
At the centre of Beijing’s position is respect for national sovereignty. China, he said, believes that the sovereignty, security and territorial integrity of Iran and all countries in the Gulf must be respected.
He also condemned what he called the abuse of force in international relations.
“Might does not make right,” he said. “The law of the jungle must not return to rule the world.”
Mr. Wang further urged non-interference in domestic affairs, arguing that the countries of the Middle East should determine their own future without outside attempts at regime change.
“The people of the Middle East are the true masters of the region,” he said.
Beijing also renewed its call for political dialogue, encouraging all parties to return to negotiations and resolve disputes through diplomacy.
Finally, he urged major global powers to act responsibly in promoting stability.
“Major countries should act in the spirit of justice and righteousness,” he said, adding that they must contribute “positive energy” toward peace and development in the region.
China, he added, stands ready to cooperate with regional partners under its Global Security Initiative to restore stability and peace.
Beyond geopolitical tensions, Mr. Wang devoted significant attention to the upcoming Asia-Pacific Economic Cooperation leaders’ summit, which China will host later this year.
The November meeting in Shenzhen will mark the third time Beijing has hosted the forum, following summits in Shanghai in 2001 and Beijing in 2014.
Mr. Wang said this year’s summit will revolve around the theme “Building an Asia Pacific Community to Prosper Togetheemphasisingzing openness, innovation and cooperation.
According to the Chinese foreign minister, the gathering aims to transform the long-discussed idea of an Asia-Pacific community into concrete action.
“For decades, this vision has remained largely on paper,” he said. “We hope to find an answer in Shenzhen that delivers consensus, clear priorities and workable steps.”
Chinese authorities plan to host more than 300 events nationwide in the run-up to the summit, with participation from multiple provinces and all APEC member economic
Mr. Wang described Shenzhen as a fitting venue for the summit, portraying the southern technology hub as a symbol of China’s economic transformation.
“Shenzhen is an important window on China’s reform and opening up and a frontier of innovation,” he said.
The twin messages—calling for peace in the Middle East while championing economic cooperation in the Asia-Pacific—highlight China’s effort to project itself as botstabilisingzing diplomatic actor and a central driver of regional development.
Analysts say Beijing’s rhetoric underscores its growing ambition to shape global governance at a time when geopolitical tensions are deepening across several regions.
For China, the upcoming APEC summit represents not only a diplomatic milestone but also an opportunity to showcase its vision for economic integration and technological transformation in the Asia-Pacific. Mr Wang put it, the goal is to use the Shenzhen summit as a new starting point.
“We stand ready,” he said, “to work with all sides to chart the course toward an Asia-Pacific community and shared prosperity.”

Court Impounds Basse Area Council Vehicles Over Loan Owed to Supersonicz Financial
Latest News
Legal Affairs
By Lamin Fatty
Officials from the Sheriff Division of the High Court on Tuesday impounded vehicles belonging to the Basse Area Council and transported them to the court premises in Basse, enforcing a judgment linked to a D10 million loan facility granted to the council by Supersonicz Financial Limited, now called Salam.
The enforcement action, carried out under the supervision of the Sheriff Division of the High Court of The Gambia, followed a legal case filed in May 2025 after the council allegedly failed to settle the remaining balance from the loan obtained in 2021. Court officials moved to attach council assets, including multiple vehicles, which were subsequently taken to the court compound in Basse as part of the recovery process.
The development marks one of the most dramatic enforcement actions against a local government authority in recent years, highlighting the growing financial pressures faced by some councils across the country.
In a public statement issued after the incident, the council acknowledged the court action but said the D10 million loan was contracted under a previous administration, leaving the current leadership to grapple with the financial consequences.
“The present administration inherited several liabilities and debt burdens from the previous administration, including this loan,” the council said, stressing that it had since assumed full responsibility for addressing the outstanding obligations.
Officials noted that since taking office, they had made significant payments toward the debt, reducing the amount owed to the lender to D5,049,107. Despite those repayments, however, the legal proceedings initiated by the lender culminated in the enforcement order that led to the seizure of the council’s vehicles.
Council leaders said the situation has generated public debate in the Upper River Region, with some residents questioning the financial decisions that led to the dispute. The administration, however, maintained that the circumstances surrounding the loan predate its tenure.
“We have consistently explained to the people of the region the financial realities we inherited and the reasons reforms became necessary,” the council said in its statement.
Those reforms, the council said, are part of a broader effort to restore accountability, strengthen financial discipline and combat corruption within the institution responsible for administering development programmes across the region.
Under the Local Government Act of 2002, councils in The Gambia are mandated to exercise political and executive authority over a wide range of local services, including sanitation, markets, community development initiatives and social welfare programmes. They are also responsible for regulating trade activities in public spaces, managing local infrastructure such as parks and cemeteries, and overseeing environmental health measures.
Council officials said the weight of inherited debts has placed considerable strain on the institution’s ability to fully deliver on those responsibilities, limiting the resources available for development initiatives.
Despite the enforcement action, however, sources within the council say discussions are now underway with Supersonicz Financial Limited to reach a mutually agreeable payment settlement arrangement.
Officials familiar with the negotiations said council representatives are engaging the financial institution to determine a structured repayment plan that could eventually lead to the release of the impounded vehicles and the resolution of the dispute.
At the same time, the council said it remains focused on strengthening internal governance structures. In recent months, it has introduced a number of transparency and accountability measures, including placing several senior officials on administrative leave over alleged financial mismanagement.
The council also called on journalists, commentators and the public to approach the matter with accuracy, fairness and responsibility, warning against speculation or misleading interpretations of the events surrounding the loan dispute.
“We urge the public and the media to reflect the facts of the case and the context in which these developments have occurred,” the statement said.
While negotiations continue, the administration insists that its long-term priority remains restoring financial stability and ensuring the council can meet its obligations to both creditors and the communities it serves.
“We remain committed to transparency, reform and responsible leadership as we work to restore stability and deliver meaningful development for the people of the Upper River Region,” the council said, adding that efforts to address inherited debts are ongoing.

ROOTS Project Hosts National Policy Dialogue on Boosting Local Onion Marketing
Food & Agriculture
The Resilience of Organizations for Transformative Small Holder Agriculture Project (ROOTS) convened a pivotal policy dialogue on Wednesday under the theme “National Convergence on Marketing of Locally Produced Onions in The Gambia.”
The event, held on the
15th October 2025
, brought together stakeholders from across the agricultural value chain to share experiences, address persistent challenges, and chart a way forward for the country’s onion sector.
The dialogue is part of ROOTS’ ongoing efforts to increase agricultural productivity and market access for smallholder farmers. Over the years, ROOTS has organized several such forums, aligning with its core objective of empowering local producers and boosting rural livelihoods. A key component of this strategy is the implementation of Agricultural Value Chain Interaction Platforms (AVIPs). These platforms facilitate connections and dialogue among producers, traders, microfinance institutions, and other market actors.
During the 2024/2025 onion season, AVIPs supported a series of collaborative interactions across regions, with mixed outcomes. The platforms aimed to strengthen the marketing of locally produced onions, a sector that continues to face significant hurdles despite notable progress.
In his opening remarks, ROOTS Project Director Mr. Mamour Jagne emphasized the ongoing relevance of the issue, recalling similar gatherings in the early years of the project. “We have made important strides, but persistent challenges remain for our local producers. This dialogue is an opportunity for all stakeholders to directly address those issues and find sustainable solutions,” he said.
Representing the Ministry of Trade, Deputy Permanent Secretary Dr. Jerry Sanyang highlighted the economic stakes, noting that The Gambia spends around 15 million dollars annually on onion imports. “This is not a reliance we can afford in the long term. Protecting local production and farmers is a top priority, and the ministry has introduced market moratoriums for that purpose,” Dr. Sanyang explained, urging participants to exchange ideas and experiences to overcome barriers in local onion marketing.
Speaking on behalf of the Ministry of Agriculture, Dr. Saikou Sanyang underscored agriculture’s central role in national development. “For food security, nutrition, and poverty reduction, agriculture must be at the heart of our policies. The Ministry is shifting from subsistence to commercialization, and this policy dialogue is essential for strategizing how farmers can gain reliable access to markets,” he stated.
The forum aims to build on experiences from the last marketing season, fostering a coordinated response to ensure the success of the 2025/2026 dry season onion production. By uniting diverse voices and expertise, the ROOTS project hopes to reduce post-harvest losses, stabilize prices, improve farmer incomes, and strengthen the contribution of onion farming to national food and nutrition security.

Home-based Scorpions share spoils with Algeria in CHAN playoff
Latest News
Sports
Home-based Scorpions are yet to score a goal in the qualifiers. © GFFMedia.
By Momodou Jarju
The Algerian local-based national team Saturday held The Gambia’s Home-based Scorpions to a goalless draw in the first leg of the TotalEnergies African Nations Championships (CHAN) final qualifying round.
The afternoon kick off was held at the newly refurbished Independence Stadium in Bakau—a first home game played in Gambian soil after little over three years of the country playing home games away due to CAF ban on the aforesaid stadium. The turnout was poor by the way!
The game’s tempo was quite slow in intensity as the visitors gave the hosts the ball possession for the most part while they tried to create goal scoring situations amid a weather condition they aren’t accustomed to.
Both teams started the game with slow build ups and two minutes into the first half saw Gambia’s Ebrima Fofana’s strike outside the box stumbled into the path of striker Ebrima Gaye who, oblivious of the situation, couldn’t capitalise on the slim opportunity. Fofana attempted another strike out of the 18-yard but it was weak to trouble the Algerian goalkeeper Oussama Benbot moments later.
The visitors grew into the game in the first quarter getting three corners in the process to The Gambia’s none. Gambian shot-stopper Lamin Saidy received his first test of the game when he saved forward Aymen Mahious’s acrobatic overhead kick at the edge of the penalty box from their second corner.
The hosts’ first corner came in the 26th minute but like their opponents, they failed to capitalise on the set piece. The Gambia also grew into the game in the last quarter of the first stanza and Adama Kanteh came close to scoring when his powerful volley out of the penalty box went inches over the crossbar in the 35th minute.
The best chance in the first half came three minutes later when the visitors’ Adil Boulbina dribbled past Captain Siady but his strike hit the near post. A minute before the end of the first stanza, Boulbina was booked for diving in the penalty box. The second period resumed with a similar start to the first. Both coaches made substitutions to strengthen their sides.
The hosts had the best chance in the 56 minute as Pa Modou Sohna dazzled with the ball and found Kanteh whose cross was headed back to his path, but his overpowered volley went over the bar. From this point, the Gambian side continued to pile more pressure on the visitors, winning possessions after possessions but failed to create threatening chances. The visitors had a chance in the 65th minute when Mahious’s header in the box went wide.
Kanteh was stretched out in the 79th minute but he went back into the field after the medics treated him briefly. He was however replaced by Ebou Camara four minutes later. The Gambia had a late chance when Sohna dribbled his marker in the right flank and delivered a low cross but the visitors cleared the danger ball after Musa Ceesay couldn’t connect to it. Thereafter, Ebou Camara almost stole the show with a last minute effort as he attempted to lob Benbot to no avail.
Meanwhile, the result leaves the tie wide open for either team in the second leg this Saturday in Algeria. The winner qualifies to the CHAN finals to be hosted jointly by Kenya, Tanzania, and Uganda from 2–30 August 2025.
The head coach of Home-based Scorpions Alhagie Sarr said they failed to capitalize on their chances and though not an excuse, they couldn’t get all players together during weeks of their training due to the ongoing domestic competitions.
“We want to win today’s game. And we want to qualify because that is our objective,” he said while noting that they’ll prepare for the return leg. Sarr said they’ll change their time of training to 7pm due to the different weather conditions in Algeria.
His counterpart Madjid Bougherra praised his players’ performance for being disciplined and having a strong mentality. He said The Gambia is “an athletic strong team” so they’re happy with the draw especially with hot weather conditions and a pitch he opined they couldn’t practise their football properly.
“It’s all the time a good result when you don’t lose. And the thing is it was the strategy of this game to leave the ball to the Gambian team. Because when you see the pitch, it’s not possible to play football properly as we want. And the tactic was to wait, to stay compact, let them open the space, and go into counterattack.
“I know if we start to press high, if we want to play football, we will suffer very fast and be tired very fast. And this is what I don’t want. And the plan was okay. We have some situations. Sometimes one situation is enough to kill the game, but we don’t score. But in the end, I’m still happy about the attitude of the player and the result,” Bougherra said. He also added that though the new look Independence Stadium is wonderful, its pitch needs improvement.

WHAT IS HAPPENING WITH THE RESHUFFLING IMPASSE
Question of The Day
The president tried to reshuffle his cabinet by moving the minister of trade to defence and the Auditor General to Trade. The Auditor General however, declined the offer. This has brought about the crisis that leaves the Ministry of trade without a holder and the Auditor General without an office. What is going to happen next is still unanswered. The opportunity now exists for the government to go back to the drawing board and do what is best for the country.
The Attorney General is the principal legal adviser of the government. It is anticipated that the government will seek and receive good advice before drawing any conclusion on this matter.

WHEN WILL THE PRICE OF GROUNDNUTS BE ANNOUNCED?
Question of The Day
November is fast approaching and despite the late rains, some farmers have harvested their crops. If the government fails to announce the producer price, the temptation to sell groundnuts across the border will increase. Some will prefer selling their nuts to weekly markets organized all over the country, called ‘lumos’. It is not clear how many tonnes are to be bought and transformed into groundnut oil and how many tonnes are to be sold as decorticated nuts or not.
Foroyaa will contact both the GGC and the Ministry of Agriculture Livestock and Food Security to find out the level of preparedness for the 2025/2026 marketing season.

Indian Cough Syrup Under Scrutiny After Child Fatalities
Health
By Nelson Manneh
In a central Indian state, nine children have died under troubling circumstances, prompting authorities to suspect that a commonly used cough syrup may be to blame. The Indian Health Ministry confirmed that samples of Coldrif Cough Syrup, produced by Sresan Pharma in Tamil Nadu, contained diethylene glycol (DEG) levels above legally permissible limits.
Initial testing by local regulators in Madhya Pradesh had examined 13 samples, finding three free of contamination. However, when state drug authorities conducted tests on samples directly sourced from the Sresan manufacturing site in Kanchipuram, Tamil Nadu, contamination was confirmed.
In response to the findings, the Indian government launched inspections of 19 drug manufacturers across six states to identify flaws in quality control and prevent similar tragedies from occurring.
The incident has drawn renewed attention to India’s pharmaceutical exports, which have previously come under international scrutiny. In 2022, a World Health Organization investigation linked certain Indian-manufactured cough syrups to the deaths of approximately 70 children in The Gambia. Both cases highlight the dangers posed by contaminated pediatric medicines and underscore the importance of rigorous quality control measures.
While the Indian case involves domestic fatalities linked to a single manufacturer, the Gambian tragedy demonstrated the far-reaching consequences of unsafe pharmaceutical imports, where contaminated products caused mass casualties in a foreign country. Health experts say the two incidents underscore a critical need for stronger regulatory oversight, both within India and among importing nations, to ensure that children are protected from toxic medicines.

Question of The Day
WHAT IS THE ROLE OF THE YOUTH IN POLITICS?
Question of The Day
This question was posed to Halifa Sallah and he responded as follows.
It is important to address this question from a point of age, law and political science.
In terms of age, every Gambian becomes a sovereign person at birth and has a right to survival, protection and development. It is the duty of society to educate and nurture the person to be a sovereign citizen from the cradle, based on the level of the physical, mental, emotional and social development of the child.
At each stage of development of a child one should know one’s rights and duties to self, country and people and quantitatively extending to continent, and humanity in order to facilitate holistic development.
Law indicates that anybody under 18 years is a child. Even though the supreme law of the land indicates that sovereignty relies in each citizen and that the authority to govern must be derived from the consent of the people and exercised to promote their general welfare, and further guarantees political rights to participate directly or indirectly in managing the affairs of society as electors or elected persons, it restricts those under 18 years from being voters.
Hence children under 18 have the right to freedom of expression and association to say how they wish their country to serve them and prepare them to serve society to mutual benefits, and should therefore be given all the civic education necessary to be able to influence policies and institutions of the state, especially the government. At the age of 18 the child grows up to be a youth who does not only have a right to freedom of expression and association to influence policies and institutions but also the right to decide on how policies and institutions should be formulated and built respectively to serve them by exercising their right to vote.
At the age of 18 the youth must become very conscious that an individual decision does not determine policies and the building of institutions. The decision of everyone   must be collected to become the decision of the majority in order to have the authority to determine who should be their public trustees who would be charged with the responsibility to provide them with the policies and institutions they deserve and demand.
Hence every youth who is a nurtured sovereign citizen should have the political maturity to know that he or she is embodied with three powers – the power to say what policies and institutions are fit for society; the power to decide how to bring about the right policies and institutions and the power to determine in collaboration with other sovereign citizens the types of policies and institutions that they should have to build a country fit for them.
In the exercise of these three powers the youth must become a progressive activist who would first seek to know the type of policies and institutions that would lead to the building of a freer, fairer, better and more prosperous society and then advocate for the unity of the sovereign people to  bring about the type of government that will implement such policies and build such institutions.
As the youth at 18 gain this maturity without being able to stand for elections for councils until they are 21 years old, they would have acquired the maturity through progressive activism to be ready for representation in councils and the National Assembly or make informed choice as to who should represent them.
The law still restricts participation in elections to the highest of the land until they are 30 which is the minimum age to contest in a presidential election. Hence, the progressive activists should be able to gain knowledge, experience and moral conviction to prepare self to be mature, should be the one capable of building a country of liberty , dignity and prosperity to her people.
This is the type of youth that Africa and the world must nurture to have democracy, peace and development for the future of all. A better society with a self-reliant economy can only be built by people of the required knowledge, experience and self-abnegation.
In my view, we should be wary of populist activism that is now reigning all over the world.
Young people who are famous for one reason would simply want to moblise people to climb the highest ladder of political representation. They need self-abnegation to build a better society for the benefit of all. I sympathise with many young people who are dissatisfied with the way things are. However, if I look at how African leaders are relating to President Trump, I would caution that those who wish to occupy the highest office should know that the terrain is a very rough one. They should not wear kids’ gloves to get to the arena to fight for their country and the destiny of their people. World leadership is not a child’s game. It requires maturity of the highest degree to weather the storm and stress of our time.

WHAT IS THE END RESULT OF THE SKYROCKETING OF VISA FEES?
Question of The Day
The world is quiet but the movement of goods, services and people are gradually being restricted by protectionist measures that could lead to the collapse of the world economy and the restriction of movement from one country  to the other on the basis of the few who have the money to pay the visa fee. It will also encourage those who do not have the money to move to the drug trade and other illicit ways of earning to get the money for visa fees. Hopelessness may also drive the young into terrorist organisations that may pose to be fighting the domination of the world by the few. We are entering a dangerous new world and instead of reciprocating, African leaders should meet to come with common decisions on how to reverse the use of increasing visa fees to restrict legal migration.

NPP Councillor Abandons Post, Forcing By-Election in Half Die Ward
Politics
By Nelson Manneh
The Independent Electoral Commission (IEC) has scheduled a by-election for
April 26, 2025
, to fill the vacant
Half Die Ward
council seat in Banjul after
Momodou Bah
, the National People’s Party (NPP) councillor, abandoned his position and disappeared abroad.
Bah, who won the 2023 election with
63.90% of the vote
, reportedly left for Europe and failed to return. With no official excuse for his prolonged absence, the IEC declared his seat vacant, invoking
Section 23 of the Local Government Act
to initiate a fresh election.
The IEC has outlined the following key dates for the by-election:
March 12:
Nomination forms available at the IEC Banjul Regional Office.
April 10-12:
Official nomination period (8 a.m. – 4 p.m. daily).
April 14-24:
Campaign period for candidates.
April 26:
Election day.
Bah’s disappearance has created a political headache for the
NPP
, which must now defend a seat it won decisively against
Ruggie Faal
of the United Democratic Party (UDP) in 2023. Bah secured
661 votes
, while Faal received
374 votes (36.10%)
.
His abrupt departure has triggered public criticism, with many questioning the
commitment and accountability of elected officials
. Some residents are calling for stricter regulations to prevent similar cases of abandonment in the future.
The IEC has assured the public that the by-election will be conducted
fairly and transparently
, encouraging eligible voters to actively participate in choosing a
representative who will fulfill their mandate
.
With the nomination period fast approaching, political parties are gearing up for what is expected to be a
fiercely contested race
to fill the vacant Half Die Ward seat.

WHAT IS THE GOVERNMENT PLAN TO TACKLE YOUTH UNEMPLOYMENT?
Question of The Day
The youth population is quite high, and youth unemployment is also quite high. One always sees unemployed youth in groups hanging around during working hours. Their situation leads them to risk the perilous journey to seek greener pasture, leading to many of them dying at sea.
It is the responsibility of the government to facilitate the employment of hundreds of thousands of our youth. Foroyaa will find out from the authorities what their plans and policies are for youth employment.

WILL FUEL PRICES CONTINUE TO BE ERATIC?
Question of The Day
Fuel, like bread and rice, are inflation engineering commodities. The more their prices rise, the more many other commodities and services that are linked to them will also rise in cost and prices. For example, as fuel pric3es increase. One would expect drivers to increase fares. Such fares would have impact on all other commodities that are transported to the markets of the country or the cost of services that utilise fuel for generation capacity.
There are many ways that fuel prices could be managed. Foroyaa will engage the ministry of trade to find out how fuel subsidies or their absence are affecting the price fluctuation of fuel. The ministry of trade has been intervening to manage transport fares in light of rises in the price of fuel. The ministry of petroleum, energy and mines will require close collaboration with the ministry of trade to address the problem of price fluctuation and appreciation.

Court Sentences Two Men to Five Years in Prison in Homosexuality Case
Latest News
Legal Affairs
By Mariama Marong
The Brusubi Magistrates’ Court on Thursday sentenced two men to five years in prison after convicting them of engaging in what the law describes as “indecent practice between males.” Delivering her judgment in a packed courtroom, Magistrate Isatou Jallow found Muhammad Jawo and Muhammad Krubally guilty under Section 147(1) of the country’s Criminal Code, a provision that criminalises acts of “gross indecency” between male persons.
The case, which unfolded over several hearings in recent months, has drawn attention not only for the severity of the sentence imposed but also because the magistrate herself described it as “the first of its kind” she had presided over.
The two accused persons stood before the court facing allegations that they had engaged in homosexual acts on 18 May 2025 in Old Yundum, in the West Coast Region. Prosecutors alleged that the men deliberately sought out an isolated location, which was an unfinished building along a back road near a cemetery, where they engaged in sexual acts. According to the particulars of the offence presented in court, the men were found performing acts of oral sex on each other, a conduct that the prosecution argued constituted “gross indecency” under Gambian law.
During the trial, the prosecution, led by Inspector S. Kebbeh, appearing on behalf of the Inspector General of Police, called three witnesses and presented exhibits to support its case. Addressing the court in his submissions, Inspector Kebbeh insisted the prosecution had successfully met the required legal standard.
“The prosecution has proven its case beyond a reasonable doubt,” he told the court.
The defence, led by counsel M.L. Drammeh, strongly contested the prosecution’s claims. In his closing address, Drammeh argued that the evidence brought before the court was insufficient to sustain a conviction.
He reminded the court of the constitutional principle that an accused person is presumed innocent until proven guilty, citing Section 24(3) of the 1997 Constitution. He also referred the magistrate to Section 144 of the Evidence Act, emphasising that the prosecution must establish guilt beyond a reasonable doubt in criminal proceedings.
“The prosecution has failed to prove its claims,” Drammeh argued. “The law is clear where evidence falls short of the required standard.”
Following the conviction, Drammeh appealed to the court to show leniency.  In a plea in mitigation, he stressed that the men were first-time offenders and had demonstrated respect throughout the proceedings.
“The convicts have been punctual and respectful to the court,” he said. “Their family members have also consistently attended proceedings to show support and respect for the court.”
He urged the magistrate to temper justice with mercy.
Magistrate Jallow, however, ruled that the prosecution had proven the offence beyond a reasonable doubt. In a detailed judgment, she cited Section 147(1) of the Criminal Code (Cap 10:01) of the Revised Laws of The Gambia (2009), which states that a male person who commits an act of gross indecency with another male person, whether in public or private, commits a felony punishable by five years’ imprisonment. The law further states in Section 147(3) that an act of gross indecency includes any homosexual act.
“The mandatory sentence of anyone convicted of this offence is five years’ imprisonment — no more, no less,” Magistrate Jallow said.
She noted that while courts may, in certain circumstances, apply Section 34 of the Criminal Offences Act to exercise discretion in sentencing, such discretion must be used “judicially and judiciously.”
In explaining her decision, Magistrate Jallow said the circumstances surrounding the case suggested the acts were premeditated. She said the two men had deliberately chosen a remote location.
“They intentionally took the back road of Old Yundum, which is isolated, and carefully chose an incomplete building close to the cemetery,” she told the court.
According to her ruling, the men willingly engaged in acts that constituted gross indecency under the Criminal Code. The magistrate also said the court had taken note of the behaviour of the accused during the trial.
“I have taken judicial notice of the demeanour of the convicts in court and their behaviour towards each other even while in the dock,” she said.
She added that the court had twice cautioned them to exercise restraint and behave appropriately.
“They do not seem to have any remorse for what they did,” she concluded.
In a notable portion of the judgment, Magistrate Jallow acknowledged broader debates surrounding gender identity and LGBT rights globally.
“This court has strong regard for the fundamental principles of human rights and is aware of rights to gender identification and LGBT rights among others,” she said.
However, she stressed that Gambian law and social norms must be taken into account.
“In this jurisdiction, unlike others, homosexuality is not legalised nor is any act deemed indecent between persons of the same gender,” she said.
The magistrate added that courts operate as representatives of society and must consider prevailing moral and cultural standards.
“I have also reminded myself that courts sit in place of the Gambian society,” she said. “Which I do not believe is ready now to see its sons engaging in acts of homosexuality.”
Magistrate Jallow also emphasised the role of the criminal justice system in deterrence. She stated that the actions of the men posed risks to societal norms and moral standards.
“The introduction of the actions of the convicts into the communities poses huge risks to societal norms and raises questions of morality,” she said.
“Whether such acts occur in public or private, they pose risks to society.”
She added that the seriousness of the offence could not be overlooked.
The magistrate also criticised the defendants for pleading not guilty and contesting the charges. She said their stance had prolonged the trial.
“The convicts wasted the court’s time by pleading not guilty to the charge,” she said.
As a result, she declined to apply Section 34 of the Criminal Offences Act in their favour to reduce the sentence. Instead, she imposed the sentence prescribed by law. Magistrate Jallow concluded by formally sentencing the two men.
“Muhammad Jawo and Muhammad Krubally are hereby sentenced to serve five years’ imprisonment,” she ruled.
The conviction was entered under Section 147(1) of the Criminal Code, which criminalises indecent practices between male persons. The case centres on the events of 18 May 2025, when the two men were found engaging in acts described by the court as gross indecency in Old Yundum.

Army Sergeant Admits Cannibalization of Government Vehicles
Your Representative
By Kebba AF Touray
Sergeant Adama Jagne of The Gambia Armed Forces on Tuesday, 20th October 2025, admitted before the National Assembly’s Special Select Committee that he cannibalized government vehicles parked at the Mechanical Services Agency (MSA) in Kotu.
Cannibalization of vehicles refers to the practice of removing parts from one vehicle to repair or maintain another, often leaving the stripped vehicle unusable.
He cited that the vehicles involved included one from the National Disaster Management Agency, one from the National Sports Council, a pickup, and a van.
Jagne made the admission during his reappearance before the Special Select Committee probing the disposal and sale of assets identified by the Janneh Commission as properties belonging to former President Yahya A.J.J. Jammeh.
He told the committee that the vehicles were parked at the MSA and were earmarked for auction.
Counsel Lamin M. Dibba asked him to explain how he knew the categories of vehicles on his first day at MSA when “you did not meet Nfamara Saidybah that first day.” Dibba also told him that Saidybah’s letter denied ever briefing him about the vehicles, stating that he only met Jagne one day in November and found out that he had already removed parts from most of the vehicles.
In response, Sgt. Jagne said, “I cannibalize, and I will never deny it. And even if I cannibalize, if you ask me, I will tell you why I cannibalized,” but denied Saidybah’s statement that he was not briefed about the vehicles at the MSA.
At the start of his testimony, Counsel Dibba asked Jagne about his relationship or any interactions with former President Yahya Jammeh and whether he had ever conducted errands for him. Jagne replied in the negative, stating that he had no relationship with the former President and had never conducted any errand for him.
Counsel Dibba then told him that according to records, one officer named Adama Jagne was a close confidant of the former President and was allegedly assigned to deposit money for him. Reacting to this, Sgt. Jagne said, “No, I am not that person.”
He explained that the Adama Jagne referred to was a senior officer at the time—“I think by then she was a captain, but actually, I don’t know what rank she carried. She was working directly with the army, called Adama Jagne 4670.”
When asked about his relationship with the MSA before the incident, Jagne said he had never worked there until some people he referred to as “Coalition people” attempted to access vehicles from the agency.
Dibba asked him what motivated him to act, given that he was not in charge of MSA at the time.
Jagne explained that he intervened because the people he confronted were civilians who were not working for the government but were attempting to take vehicles from the MSA. “As far as I know, I am a security personnel. That warranted me to go and stop them. It was a personal decision,” he said.
He added that he took up the responsibility after seeing one of the people, identified as Sanna, driving a vehicle allegedly taken from General Basseng. According to him, Sanna later brought the vehicle to the MSA for repairs, and when questioned about its ownership, Sana “just smiled.” Jagne said he then called one Saidybah, who instructed him to impound the vehicle.
He said the vehicle was later taken by former State Guard Commander Turo Jawneh, who “gave it to his wife, and she still uses the vehicle.”

Gambia Will Not Renew Fisheries Agreement With European Union, Minister Tells Parliament
Your Representative
By Kebba AF Touray
The Minister of Fisheries told lawmakers on Wednesday that the country has no plans to renew its long-standing fishing agreement with the European Union, marking the end of a partnership that had shaped relations between the two sides for nearly four decades.
“The Gambia EU Fishing Agreement expired on the 30th July 2025 and has not been renewed,” Minister Musa Drammeh said during a question-and-answer session in Parliament.
The issue was raised by Hon. Musa Cham of Serrekunda, who asked: “Could the Minister update the assembly on the current status of the Gambia-EU Fishing Agreement?”
Drammeh’s response was unequivocal. “There is no intention of renewing it because the first contract was not favourable to us and even the EU is not interested in renewing the agreement, because they are also under pressure from their parliamentarians,” he told the chamber.
He added that European lawmakers had voiced dissatisfaction with the deal. “They are not getting the value from the money they are giving us,” he said.
The announcement brought closure to a fisheries partnership dating back to 1987. The Sustainable Fisheries Partnership Agreement (SFPA) between the EU and The Gambia, which entered into force on July 31, 2019, was designed to last six years with automatic renewal.
Its most recent protocol ended on July 30, 2025. Even before expiry, the agreement had been described as “dormant,” meaning that while the partnership formally existed, there was no active protocol governing catches and financial contributions. Under such conditions, the EU fleet was barred from fishing in Gambian waters, and direct licensing was prohibited under the exclusivity clause of the common fisheries policy.
Hon. Assan Touray of Bakau pressed the minister further, asking whether the agreement might still be renewed. Drammeh restated the government’s position. “There is no intention of renewing it,” he said firmly.
For The Gambia, the minister suggested, the agreement did not deliver enough benefits to justify its continuation. For the European Union, he added, pressure from within its own Parliament made renewal politically unattractive.
With both sides signalling disinterest, the end of the agreement seemed assured.
